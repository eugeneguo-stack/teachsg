# Differentiation
[stextbox caption="Differentiation Rules" stextbox id="default"]$1.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} c = 0$
$2.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} x^n = nx^{n-1} $
$3.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \sin x = \cos  x $
$4.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \cos x = -\sin  x $
$5.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \tan x = \sec^2 x $
$6.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} e^{x} = e^{x} $
$7.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \ln\; x = \frac{1}{x}$
Note: $\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} k\text{f}(x)=k\times\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \text{f}(x)$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Chain Rule" stextbox id="download"]For $y=\text{f}(u)$ and $u=\text{g}(x)$,
$\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}u} \times \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}$
[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Further Differentiation Rules (Chain Rule)" stextbox id="info"]$1.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} (ax+b)^n = an(ax+b)^{n-1} $
$2.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \sin (ax+b) = a\cos  (ax+b)$
$3.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \cos (ax+b) = -a\sin  (ax+b) $
$4.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \tan (ax+b) = a\sec^2 (ax+b) $
$5.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} e^{ax+b} = ae^{ax+b} $
$6.\,\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \ln\; (ax+b) = \frac{a}{ax+b}$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Product Rule" stextbox id="default"]$\dfrac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x}(uv) = u\dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x} + v\dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Quotient Rule" stextbox id="download"]$\dfrac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x}\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)=\dfrac{v\frac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}-u\frac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x}}{v^2}$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Gradient Of Curve" stextbox id="info"]From $y=\text{f}(x)$,
$\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$ is the gradient of the curve.[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Gradient Of Tangent & Normal" stextbox id="default"]From $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$, substitute $x=k$ to get $m$ (gradient of tangent).
$-\dfrac{1}{m}$ is the gradient of the normal.[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Increasing & Decreasing Functions" stextbox id="download"]1. For increasing functions, $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}>0\\$.
2. For decreasing functions, $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}<0$.[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Rates Of Change" stextbox id="info"]$\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}t}=\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}\times\dfrac{\text{d}x}{\text{d}t}$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="First Derivative Test" stextbox id="default"]If $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}=0$ for $x=k$, test for $k^-$, $k$, $k^+$.

Maximum point:
$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
x & k^- & k & k^+\\\hline
\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} & + & 0 & - \\ \hline
\end{array}$$

Minimum point:
$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
x & k^- & k & k^+\\\hline
\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} & - & 0 & + \\ \hline
\end{array}$$

Inflexion point:
$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
x & k^- & k & k^+\\\hline
\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} & + & 0 & + \\\hline
\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} & - & 0 & -  \\\hline
\end{array}$$[/stextbox]

[stextbox caption="Second Derivative Test" stextbox id="download"]1. If $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}<0\\$, it is a maximum point.
2. If $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}>0$, it is a minimum point.
3. If $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}=0$, need to do first derivative test. 
[/stextbox]
